In the yoga tradition, particularly in Patanjali's Yoga Sutras, concentration and meditation are not the same thing. They are actually two distinct stages on the same path, and understanding the difference can transform your practice.

## Concentration (Dharana)

Dharana means "focusing the mind on a single point." In concentration, you deliberately choose an object—your breath, a mantra, a candle flame, or even a concept like compassion—and hold your attention there.

The mind will resist. It will want to wander. Your job is to gently bring it back, again and again. This takes effort. This is the practice of concentration.

Concentration is like training a puppy. You keep bringing it back to where you want it to be. The puppy will wander again. You bring it back again. Over time, the wandering happens less frequently.

## Meditation (Dhyana)

Dhyana is what happens when concentration becomes effortless. When you can hold your attention on the chosen object without struggle, without the sense of "I am concentrating," you have moved from dharana into dhyana.

In meditation, the meditator and the object of meditation begin to merge. The boundaries between subject and object dissolve. This is not a trance or a special state—it's a natural deepening of presence.

## The Practical Difference

Here's a simple way to understand it:

- In concentration, you do something: you focus, you hold, you control
- In meditation, something happens: the boundaries relax, the doing becomes being

Most beginners spend months or years in the concentration phase, and this is perfectly fine. Concentration is the foundation. Without the ability to hold attention, meditation cannot arise.

## How to Progress

The key is not to force yourself into meditation. Let concentration develop naturally. Sit regularly. Practice returning your attention to your anchor. Eventually, the quality of your attention will shift from effort to ease.

This is why we practice. Not to achieve something, but to allow the natural depth of awareness to reveal itself.
