Walk into any gym or fitness studio and you'll likely find "yoga" classes offered alongside treadmill workouts and weight training. While these classes can be beneficial, they represent only a small fragment of what yoga actually is.

Yoga is not exercise. It's not about flexibility, strength, or looking a certain way. So what is it?

## The Word Itself

The Sanskrit word "yoga" comes from the root "yuj," meaning "to join" or "to unite." The traditional definition, from Patanjali's Yoga Sutras, is "chitta vritti nirodha"—the cessation of the fluctuations of the mind.

Yoga is the process of stilling the mind until it rests in its natural state of peace. This has nothing to do with how far you can stretch or how many poses you can hold.

## The Physical Practice (Asana)

In the yoga tradition, physical postures (asanas) serve a specific purpose: to prepare the body for meditation. A body that is stiff, tired, or uncomfortable becomes an obstacle to sitting still.

Asanas were never meant to be an end in themselves. They are tools—ways of preparing the body so that meditation becomes possible.

## Why This Matters Today

In the West, yoga has become synonymous with exercise. This isn't bad—physical practice can reduce stress, improve health, and create a sense of well-being. But if we stop there, we miss the deeper gift that yoga offers.

The classical purpose of yoga is liberation (moksha)—freedom from suffering, freedom from the identification with the mind and body, freedom to rest in who we truly are.

## A Deeper Invitation

If you're drawn to yoga, I invite you to explore its deeper dimensions. Start with the physical practice by all means, but let it open doors to stillness, to self-inquiry, to the recognition of your true nature.

This is what yoga is for. This is what it's always been for.
