At first glance, yoga and Vedanta might seem like different traditions. Yoga emphasizes practices—postures, breathing, concentration. Vedanta emphasizes wisdom—the understanding of who you truly are. But throughout Indian spiritual history, these paths have been seen as complementary, two sides of the same coin.

## The Goal Is the Same

Both yoga and Vedanta point toward the same destination: liberation from suffering and the realization of one's true nature.

Vedanta teaches that you are already That—Awareness itself, not a limited ego bound by body and mind. Yoga creates the conditions where this recognition can arise naturally.

## Wisdom and Practice

There's an old saying: "Wisdom (jnana) and practice (krriya) are like two wings of a bird." You need both to fly.

Vedanta provides the understanding. It tells you what you are: consciousness itself, unlimited and free. But understanding alone doesn't transform us. We need practice to prepare the mind and body.

Yoga provides the preparation. Through ethical living, physical readiness, breath control, and meditation, yoga creates the internal environment where wisdom can take root.

## My Own Path

For me, this integration has been essential. My study of Vedanta gave me the vision—my meditation practice gives me the experience. Neither alone was complete.

When I sit for meditation, Vedanta informs my understanding: the mind that wanders is not my true self. The awareness that observes the wandering—that is what I am. This understanding brings a lightness to the practice, a freedom from trying too hard.

## How to Integrate Them

You don't have to choose between wisdom and practice. Let them support each other:

- Study Vedanta teachings to understand what you're pointing toward
- Practice yoga and meditation to prepare for realization
- Let understanding inform practice, and let practice deepen understanding

This is the tradition I follow, and it's what I share with students. Whether you start with study or with practice, both paths lead to the same truth.
